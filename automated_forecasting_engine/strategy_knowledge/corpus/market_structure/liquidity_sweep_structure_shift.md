# Liquidity Sweep And Structure Shift

Use this as market-structure context, not as an automatic trading signal.

Core read:
- A swing low is a visible low where price previously turned.
- Liquidity often sits below swing lows because stops and resting orders accumulate there.
- Multiple similar lows create a larger liquidity pool below them.
- A wick below the last low can be a liquidity sweep.
- If the prior bullish structure remains valid, price should recover and respect the swept zone.
- If price only bounces weakly, retests the prior support area, then falls and closes below the low, treat that as a bearish break of structure.
- After the break of structure, consolidation can be accumulation or distribution before the next move.
- Look for alignment:
  - fair value gap left by an impulsive move,
  - former support turning into resistance,
  - uncollected downside liquidity still below.
- When those align after a bearish break, expect price may retrace up into the former support / fair value gap area, then rotate down to sweep remaining downside liquidity.

Decision use:
- Do not call a sweep bullish just because price wicks below a low.
- Confirm whether price reclaims the level or closes below structure.
- Former support acting as resistance after a break is bearish confirmation.
- Downside liquidity below equal lows can become the target after bearish structure shift.
- For long entries, avoid buying directly into the retest/resistance zone after bearish structure break.
- For short/avoid decisions, prioritize setups where liquidity sweep, break of structure, fair value gap, and support-to-resistance flip align.
- In stock/ETF advice, use this read to improve timing, invalidation, and patience. It does not override fundamentals, news, portfolio risk, or execution gates.
